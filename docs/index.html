Who are the กวย?

The Elephant People of Surin

The กวย (Kuy) are an indigenous Mon-Khmer people of northeast Thailand, with communities in Surin, Si Sa Ket, and Buriram provinces. Their language belongs to the Austroasiatic family — related to, but distinct from, Khmer.

Historically renowned as mahouts and elephant traders, the กวย have a profound cultural bond with elephants that spans centuries. The famous Surin Elephant Roundup, held each November, is rooted in กวย tradition.

Today, with approximately 300,000 speakers across Thailand, Laos, and Cambodia, the กวย language is endangered as younger generations shift toward Isan and Central Thai. This archive exists to document what remains.

Why this archive exists

Preserving a Living Language

กวย is primarily an oral language. It has no substantial written tradition, no widely available dictionary, and very few resources for outsiders who wish to learn it. As younger generations shift to Isan Thai and Central Thai, the language that carries the คvuy world — their stories, humour, and relationship with elephants — is fading.

"Every language that disappears takes with it an irreplaceable way of understanding the world."

This project documents กวย vocabulary, phrases, and cultural knowledge directly from a native speaker in Surin province. Wherever possible, recordings capture the real spoken voice — because no transcription can fully replace the sound of a language alive in a person's mouth.
